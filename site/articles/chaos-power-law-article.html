How Much Nonlinearity Does It Take to Create Chaos?

I changed one exponent in the logistic map and tracked what happened to the onset of chaos.

2026 · 8 minute read

I started with the logistic map, a standard example of how a simple recurrence can produce complicated behaviour:

\[x_{n+1}=r x_n(1-x_n).\]

The factor \(r x_n\) supplies growth and \(1-x_n\) suppresses it as \(x_n\) approaches one. Depending on \(r\), iterating the map leads to a fixed point, cycles of increasing period, or chaos.

Changing the suppressing term

I wanted to know what would happen if the suppressing term were not linear in \(x_n\). I replaced it with a power:

\[x_{n+1}=r x_n\left(1-x_n^p\right), \qquad p\geq 1.\]

Setting \(p=1\) recovers the usual map. For larger \(p\), \(x^p\) stays near zero through most of \((0,1)\) and rises sharply near one. Growth is restrained later and more abruptly. My question was whether that change would move the value of \(r\) at which chaos begins.

Finding the boundary of chaos

I used the Lyapunov exponent rather than judging the orbit by eye. It measures the average rate at which nearby trajectories separate.

For the generalized map, the derivative is

\[f'(x)=r\left[1-(p+1)x^p\right].\]

Following a long orbit, I estimated

\[\lambda=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\ln\left|r\left[1-(p+1)x_n^p\right]\right|.\]

Negative \(\lambda\) indicates convergence; positive \(\lambda\) indicates exponential separation and therefore chaos.

For each \(p\), I swept across \(r\), discarded the transient part of the orbit, and found the first change from a nonpositive to a positive exponent. I then used bisection to refine that crossing.

Chaos arrives earlier

For \(p=1\), the calculation returns the familiar threshold near \(r_c\approx 3.57\). The threshold drops quickly as \(p\) increases.

The decline slows for larger \(p\), and the numerical values appear to approach one:

\[r_c(p)\longrightarrow 1 \qquad \text{as}\qquad p\longrightarrow\infty.\]

My first calculation did not show this. I had searched over a coarse grid of \(r\) values, and the result appeared to flatten near \(1.1\). When I increased the resolution and refined each crossing, the plateau moved. It was an artifact of the grid, not a feature of the map.

Fitting the boundary

I tried two simple descriptions of the boundary. A power-law approach to one gave

\[r_c(p)\approx 1+\frac{3.029}{p^{1.143}}.\]

I also fitted an expansion in inverse powers of \(p\):

\[r_c(p)\approx 1+\frac{2.651}{p}-\frac{0.081}{p^2}.\]

The inverse-power model fit these numerical values better. Both fits point to the same leading-order behaviour:

\[r_c(p)-1\sim \frac{\text{constant}}{p}.\]

Why does the threshold fall?

A one-dimensional map produces chaos by stretching nearby points apart and folding them back into the interval.

For large \(p\), the term \(1-x^p\) remains close to one through most of the interval. The map therefore behaves approximately like

\[x_{n+1}\approx r x_n\]

through most of the interval. Near the upper boundary, the nonlinear correction turns on quickly. In other words, increasing \(p\) gives the orbit a long region of nearly linear stretching followed by a sharp fold. That provides a plausible reason for the lower threshold, although it is not a derivation.

What the calculation does not prove

The fitted formula is not an exact law. I measured a finite-time Lyapunov crossing, whose location depends on orbit length, numerical resolution, initial conditions, and the treatment of periodic windows.

The first positive finite-time Lyapunov exponent, the period-doubling accumulation point, and the beginning of sustained chaos are related but not identical by definition. The coefficients above should therefore be read as numerical estimates, not universal constants.

The numerical result suggests a more useful next question:

\[\text{Can one derive}\qquad r_c(p)-1\sim \frac{A}{p}\qquad \text{as}\qquad p\to\infty?\]

I do not yet have that derivation. It would be the step that turns this observed scaling into a theoretical result.

For the range I tested, increasing \(p\) moves the estimated chaos boundary toward \(r=1\), with \(r_c(p)-1\) behaving approximately like \(1/p\). The numerical evidence is clear; explaining that scaling analytically remains open.
